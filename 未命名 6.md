Solution

We have 12 objects, exactly 6 of them are “good” and the other 6 are “bad”.
A test chooses two objects; it succeeds if both are good (and then the process stops) and fails otherwise (at least one is bad).
We want the smallest number T such that there exists a deterministic strategy that, no matter which 6 objects are good, will test a good‑good pair within at most T tests.

---

Upper bound – 9 tests are enough

Partition the 12 objects into five groups

· two groups of size 3: A=\{a_1,a_2,a_3\},\;B=\{b_1,b_2,b_3\};
· three groups of size 2: C=\{c_1,c_2\},\;D=\{d_1,d_2\},\;E=\{e_1,e_2\}.

Test every pair of objects that belong to the same group.
Number of such tests:

· A: \binom{3}{2}=3 pairs,
· B: 3 pairs,
· each size‑2 group: 1 pair → 3 more.

Total 3+3+1+1+1 = 9 tests. Perform them in any order.

Why this works.
Take any set S of 6 good objects. By the pigeonhole principle, distributing 6 objects into the 5 groups forces at least one group to contain two objects of S. Those two belong to the same group, therefore the edge between them is among the 9 tested edges. When that edge is tested, both endpoints are good and the test succeeds. Hence after at most 9 tests we are sure to have found a good pair.

---

Lower bound – 9 tests are necessary

Suppose, for contradiction, that some deterministic strategy guarantees success within 8 tests.
Consider what happens when every answer we give is “failure”. Because the strategy never stops without a success, it produces an infinite sequence of tests
e_1,e_2,e_3,\dots (each e_i is an unordered pair of objects). Look only at the first eight tests e_1,\dots,e_8.
Let D be the set of distinct edges appearing among them; clearly |D|\le 8.

Construct a graph G on the 12 objects with edge set D. Its complement \overline G contains all pairs not in D; therefore

|E(\overline G)| = \binom{12}{2}-|D| \ge 66-8 = 58 .

Turán’s theorem (or the well‑known extremal result) tells us that the maximum number of edges a graph on 12 vertices can have without containing a clique of size 6 (a K_6) is 57; this maximum is achieved by the complete 5‑partite Turán graph T(12,5). Consequently, any graph with 58 or more edges must contain a K_6.
Hence \overline G contains a set S of six vertices that are pairwise adjacent – i.e. every pair of vertices in S is an edge of \overline G. Translating back, no pair of vertices of S is an edge of G; in other words, S contains none of the edges of D.

Now suppose the true set of good objects is exactly S.
Because S avoids all edges in D, each of the tests e_1,\dots,e_8 (and also any repetition of those edges) results in a failure. The strategy, receiving these eight failures, will have followed exactly the same decisions as in the hypothetical all‑fail scenario; therefore it actually asks the edges e_1,\dots,e_8 in that order and obtains only failures. After eight tests it has not succeeded – it would need a ninth test to possibly obtain a success. This contradicts the assumption that eight tests are always enough.

Thus no strategy can guarantee success with only eight tests; at least nine are required.

---

Combining the two bounds we obtain the exact value:

\boxed{9}
